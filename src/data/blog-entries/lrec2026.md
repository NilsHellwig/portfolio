This May, I had the opportunity to attend **LREC 2026 — the 15th Language Resources and Evaluation Conference** in Palma, Mallorca, Spain. The conference was held at the Palau de Congressos from May 11–16, bringing together researchers from across the NLP and language resources community. For me personally, LREC 2026 was especially exciting: together with my colleagues, we had **three papers accepted** — all of them presented as posters.

![LREC 2026 Submissions and Participating Countries](/portfolio/blog-imgs/lrec2026/lrec2026-3.png)

Getting in was not trivial. LREC has traditionally been known as a relatively accessible venue — acceptance rates often hovered above 50–60%, and submissions were based on abstracts rather than full papers. This year marked a significant shift: LREC 2026 required **full paper submissions** for the first time, attracted a record number of submissions, and with 944 accepted out of 1,786 (200 orals, 744 posters, 842 rejected), landed at an acceptance rate of **52.85%** — low by LREC's own historical standards. Presenting three papers at once was both a challenge and a genuine highlight of the conference week.

## Our Work at LREC 2026

All three papers revolve around Aspect-Based Sentiment Analysis (ABSA) — a subfield of sentiment analysis that goes beyond document-level polarity classification to identify fine-grained sentiments toward specific aspects within a text. Our contributions this year span LLM-based annotation, annotation tooling, and multilingual evaluation.

![Our Posters at LREC 2026](/portfolio/blog-imgs/lrec2026/lrec2026-2.png)

### LLM-as-an-Annotator

The first paper, **["LLM-as-an-Annotator: Training Lightweight Models with LLM-Annotated Examples for Aspect Sentiment Tuple Prediction"](https://lrec.elra.info/lrec2026-main-632)**, introduces **LA-ABSA**, a method that uses large language models as automatic annotators to reduce the reliance on costly manual labeling in ABSA.

The core idea: instead of collecting human-labeled training data, we prompt Gemma-3-27B to annotate an unlabeled corpus, then fine-tune a lightweight T5-base model (223M parameters) on the LLM-generated labels. We evaluate this across two tuple prediction tasks — Target Aspect Sentiment Detection (TASD) and Aspect Sentiment Quad Prediction (ASQP) — on five diverse datasets spanning restaurant, airline, e-learning, and hotel reviews.

Our results show that LA-ABSA achieves competitive performance with direct LLM prompting while requiring substantially less energy at inference time. Using 50 annotated in-context examples, LA-ABSA achieved an F1 score of 49.85 for ASQP on the SemEval Rest16 dataset, closely matching Gemma-3-27B ICL (51.10), while consuming only about 2.47 mWh per prediction compared to over 900 mWh for direct LLM prompting. LA-ABSA also consistently outperformed traditional data augmentation methods (EDA, QAIE, DS²-ABSA) by up to 20 percentage points in some configurations, making it a practical and energy-efficient alternative for large-scale ABSA deployments.

### AnnoABSA

The second paper, **["AnnoABSA: A Web-Based Annotation Tool for Aspect-Based Sentiment Analysis with Retrieval-Augmented Suggestions"](https://lrec.elra.info/lrec2026-main-634)**, presents the first open-source annotation tool that comprehensively supports the full spectrum of ABSA subtasks.

Existing general-purpose frameworks like Label Studio, INCEpTION, or BRAT lack ABSA-specific features such as structured tuple validation, dynamic category lists, or the ability to assign multiple aspect categories to a single text span. AnnoABSA addresses these gaps with a modern, intuitive web interface built on React.js and FastAPI. Beyond manual annotation, it introduces a **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) suggestion mechanism**: as annotation progresses, the system retrieves the ten most similar already-annotated examples via BM25 and uses them as few-shot demonstrations for an LLM (Gemma-3-27B), generating annotation suggestions that improve in quality as the labeled pool grows.

We conducted a controlled within-subjects user study with eight expert annotators and found that AI-assisted annotation reduced annotation time by **30.51%** compared to manual labeling without suggestions — a statistically significant result (p = 0.008). AnnoABSA is released under the MIT License on [GitHub](https://github.com/NilsHellwig/AnnoABSA).

### Cross-Lingual Transfer for ABSA

The third paper, **["Zero-Shot to Full-Resource: Cross-lingual Transfer Strategies for Aspect-Based Sentiment Analysis"](https://lrec.elra.info/lrec2026-main-635)**, asks: how well do state-of-the-art ABSA methods actually generalize beyond English?

We present a comprehensive multilingual evaluation across seven languages (English, German, French, Dutch, Russian, Spanish, and Czech) and four ABSA subtasks (ACD, ACSA, TASD, ASQP). We systematically compare encoder-only classifiers (BERT, Hier-GCN), sequence-to-sequence models (DLO with mT5), and decoder-only LLMs (Gemma-3-27B, LLaMA 3.1 8B) under three resource conditions: fully supervised monolingual training, zero-resource cross-lingual transfer, and data augmentation via code-switching and machine translation.

The findings reveal a clear hierarchy: fine-tuned LLaMA 3.1 8B achieves the highest overall scores, particularly for the more complex generative subtasks TASD and ASQP, while encoder-only models remain competitive on simpler classification tasks like ACD. For zero-resource settings, code-switching consistently yields the strongest relative improvements for encoder and seq-to-seq architectures. We also contribute two new German ABSA resources: an adapted GERestaurant dataset aligned to the SemEval schema, and the **first German ASQP dataset (GERest)** to enable cross-lingual opinion extraction evaluation beyond English.

## Other Interesting Papers from LREC 2026

Beyond our own work, the conference offered a wide range of contributions. Four papers that stood out to me:

### [German General Social Survey Personas: A Survey-Derived Persona Prompt Collection for Population-Aligned LLM Studies](http://www.lrec-conf.org/proceedings/lrec2026/pdf/2026.lrec2026-1.139.pdf)

Rupprecht et al. introduce **GGSS Personas**, a collection of 5,246 persona prompts grounded in the German General Social Survey (ALLBUS). The personas are designed to steer LLMs toward generating responses aligned with the German population's actual distribution of attitudes and demographics. Results show that GGSS Personas-guided LLMs outperform traditional classifiers for simulating survey response distributions, particularly under data scarcity. What I found most striking: including more persona attributes does not necessarily improve alignment — the best results came from using only the TOP-2 most informative sociodemographic variables, suggesting a clear signal-to-noise trade-off in persona design. I'm currently working with PersonaHub myself, so it was particularly interesting to see how the concept of empirically grounded persona collections is being developed further.

### [Developing the German Medical Text Corpus (GeMTeX): Legal Compliance and Semantic Enrichment](http://www.lrec-conf.org/proceedings/lrec2026/pdf/2026.lrec2026-1.122.pdf)

Hofenbitzer et al. present **GeMTeX**, a large-scale German clinical NLP resource currently comprising over 15,000 documents (20M tokens) from six university hospitals. The project follows a three-phase workflow — informed consent under GDPR, de-identification, and semantic annotation grounded in SNOMED CT — carefully navigating European data privacy law throughout. The resulting resource is among the most comprehensive German clinical corpora available.

### [Explainable Semantic Textual Similarity via Dissimilar Span Detection](http://www.lrec-conf.org/proceedings/lrec2026/pdf/2026.lrec2026-1.172.pdf)

Lozano et al. propose a new NLP task called **Dissimilar Span Detection (DSD)**: given two semantically similar sentences, identify which specific spans differ in meaning. Standard STS scores collapse all semantic nuance into a single number, so DSD adds an interpretability layer that highlights what makes two texts _not_ equivalent. The authors release the **Span Similarity Dataset (SSD)** with 1,000 semi-automatically annotated sentence pairs and evaluate LIME, SHAP, embedding-based methods, LLMs, and fine-tuned token classifiers. Incorporating DSD into a downstream paraphrase detection task improved accuracy by up to 8 percentage points — without any additional fine-tuning on the STS model itself.

### [Efficient Financial Language Understanding via Distillation with Synthetic Data](http://www.lrec-conf.org/proceedings/lrec2026/pdf/2026.lrec2026-1.804.pdf)

Huang and Simpson present a lightweight framework for **distilling instruction-following behavior from large LLMs into compact encoder-based student models** for financial sentiment analysis. The key insight is that clustering-based seed selection — using Sentence-BERT embeddings and k-means — yields more semantically diverse training examples than random sampling, which in turn leads to better synthetic data quality when expanded through structured prompting of GPT-4o. Remarkably, a ModernBERT student trained on only 105 clustered seeds plus synthetic expansion **surpassed the GPT-4o teacher** on noisy Twitter financial data, demonstrating that domain-targeted distillation can exceed generic large-model reasoning in informal financial discourse. For ABSA, this is an encouraging signal: the same principle — using a large LLM to generate diverse, domain-consistent training examples and then distilling into a compact model — is directly applicable to tuple prediction tasks like TASD or ASQP, where lightweight models are often preferred for deployment. The clustering-based seed selection strategy in particular seems worth exploring for ABSA domains with skewed aspect distributions.
